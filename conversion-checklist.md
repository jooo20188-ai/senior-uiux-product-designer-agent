# Conversion Upgrade Checklist

Purpose: Check whether a page makes its offer clear, guides the visitor to one obvious action, and earns trust without dark patterns.

How to use: Used by Mode 4 Conversion Upgrade (see [modes.md](./modes.md)). Each item is pass or fail. Mark N/A honestly when an item does not apply to the page under review. Any unchecked item becomes a finding with a severity: Critical, High Impact, Medium Impact, or Polish.

## Above the fold
- [ ] The headline states what it is and who it is for in one read.
- [ ] The subheadline adds the concrete outcome the visitor gets.
- [ ] One primary call to action is visually dominant.
- [ ] CTA text says what happens next with a specific verb, not "Submit" or "Learn more".
- [ ] A supporting visual shows the product or the outcome.
- [ ] No auto-advancing carousel holds key messages the visitor might miss.
- [ ] The value is clear without watching a video or expanding a section.
- [ ] The first screen answers "am I in the right place" for the target visitor.

## Offer clarity
- [ ] The price or a clear path to pricing is visible.
- [ ] What is included is scannable, not buried in prose.
- [ ] Who it is for, and who it is not for, is stated.
- [ ] Risk reversal is stated plainly where true, such as cancel anytime or trial length.
- [ ] The single most important benefit leads, rather than a feature list.
- [ ] Jargon is replaced with words the target visitor already uses.

## CTA hierarchy
- [ ] One primary action per screen.
- [ ] Secondary actions are visually subordinate as outline or text links.
- [ ] Competing calls to action are removed or demoted.
- [ ] The CTA repeats after each major proof section on long pages.
- [ ] A sticky or bottom-anchored CTA is present on mobile.
- [ ] The same primary action uses the same label wherever it repeats.
- [ ] The CTA stands out from surrounding elements by color and size.

## Lead capture and forms
- [ ] The form asks only for fields that are actually used.
- [ ] First contact uses email only where possible.
- [ ] Labels state why sensitive data is needed.
- [ ] The submit button describes the value received, not just "Send".
- [ ] Confirmation states what happens next and when.
- [ ] Optional fields are marked so the visitor can skip them.
- [ ] The form fits above the fold or its length is signaled up front.
- [ ] Validation errors keep the visitor's entries intact.

## Social proof and objections
- [ ] Proof sits near the claim it supports.
- [ ] Testimonials give specifics rather than generic praise.
- [ ] Objections are answered where they arise, such as cost near the pricing.
- [ ] The FAQ covers real objections, not padding.
- [ ] Proof is relevant to the target visitor, not from an unrelated audience.
- [ ] Numbers used as proof are current and can be substantiated.

## Lead capture add-ons
- [ ] A second, low-commitment action exists for visitors not ready to buy.
- [ ] The thank-you or confirmation screen sets a clear next expectation.

## Pricing clarity
- [ ] Plans are comparable at a glance.
- [ ] One recommended plan is visually marked.
- [ ] The unit of billing is explicit, such as per month or per seat.
- [ ] No hidden costs appear only at the final step.
- [ ] The difference between plans is stated in outcomes, not only feature counts.
- [ ] A yearly and monthly toggle, where offered, shows the real saving.

## Friction and decision support
- [ ] Steps to complete the primary action are counted and minimized.
- [ ] A comparison table is present where users must choose between options.
- [ ] A contact path is visible for high-consideration purchases.
- [ ] Account creation is not forced before the visitor gets value where avoidable.
- [ ] Guest checkout or a try-before-signup path exists where the model allows.
- [ ] Each form step shows progress so the visitor knows how much remains.

## Ethical guardrails
- [ ] No fake scarcity such as invented low-stock counts.
- [ ] No fake urgency timers that reset on reload.
- [ ] No confirm shaming on opt-out links.
- [ ] No pre-checked add-ons.
- [ ] No hidden auto-renewal terms.
- [ ] Any urgency or scarcity claim is true.

## Common Critical failures
- [ ] No clear primary action, so the visitor does not know what to do next.
- [ ] The headline does not say what the product is or who it serves.
- [ ] Pricing is hidden with no path to find it before commitment.
- [ ] Hidden costs are revealed only at the final checkout step.
- [ ] A fake countdown timer or invented scarcity pressures the decision.
- [ ] Confirm shaming or a pre-checked charge tricks the user into a purchase.
- [ ] The form demands sensitive data with no reason and no privacy link.
